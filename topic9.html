01

The Statics Analysis Problem

When a robot performs tasks — lifting, welding, painting, drilling — its end-effector applies a wrench (force $\mathbf{f}$ and torque $\boldsymbol{\mu}$) to the environment. The actuators at active joints must generate the corresponding torques $\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_n$ to maintain static equilibrium.

Statics problem — given end-effector wrench, find joint torques

Two main approaches are used in practice:

Method 1

Link Separation (FBD)

Isolate each link as a free body, draw all forces and torques, then apply equilibrium equations. Recursively propagate from the end-effector back to the base.

Method 2

Principle of Virtual Work

Exploit the analytic Jacobian to map end-effector wrenches directly to joint torques. Elegant, compact, and immediately connects to dynamics.

02

Link Separation Method

Isolate links and apply Newton–Euler equilibrium conditions, working from link $n$ back to link $1$.

Free-body diagram of link $i$ — forces and torques at each joint
Σ
Equilibrium Equations — Link i

Force equilibrium ($\sum\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}$):

$$\mathbf{f}_{i,i-1} - \mathbf{f}_{i+1,i} + m_i\,\mathbf{g} = \mathbf{0}$$

Moment equilibrium about $O_i$ ($\sum\boldsymbol{\mu}=\mathbf{0}$):

$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,i-1} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i+1,i} + \mathbf{r}_{O_iC_i}\times m_i\mathbf{g} - \mathbf{r}_{i,i+1}\times\mathbf{f}_{i+1,i} = \mathbf{0}$$

These give $6n$ equations for $6n$ unknown forces and joint torques.

02.2

Recursive Computation of Joint Torques

Start at the last link ($i=n$) where the boundary condition is the applied wrench, then propagate back to link $1$.

↺
Recursive Scheme (i = n, n−1, …, 1)

Boundary conditions at the end-effector:

$$\mathbf{f}_{n,n+1} = \mathbf{f}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\mu}_{n,n+1} = \boldsymbol{\mu}$$

For each link $i$ (backward from $n$ to $1$):

$$\mathbf{f}_{i,i-1} = \mathbf{f}_{i+1,i} - m_i\,\mathbf{g}$$
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,i-1} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i+1,i} - \mathbf{r}_{O_iC_i}\times m_i\mathbf{g} - \mathbf{r}_{O_iO_{i-1}}\times\mathbf{f}_{i,i-1}$$
Recursive propagation — end-effector wrench back to base
02.3

Joint Torques in Link Coordinate Frames

τ
Actuation Force / Torque Extraction

Project equilibrium equations into the local frame $O_i x_i y_i z_i$ using $\mathbf{R}^i_0$. Express vectors in the local frame (superscript $(i)$):

$$\mathbf{f}_{i,i-1}^{(i)} = \mathbf{f}_{i+1,i}^{(i)} - m_i\,\mathbf{g}^{(i)}$$
$$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,i-1}^{(i)} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i+1,i}^{(i)} - \mathbf{r}_{O_i C_i}^{(i)}\times m_i\mathbf{g}^{(i)} - \mathbf{r}_{O_i O_{i-1}}^{(i)}\times\mathbf{f}_{i,i-1}^{(i)}$$

Extract actuation torque (revolute) or force (prismatic) along $z_{i-1}$:

$$\tau_i = \begin{cases} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{i,i-1}^{(i)\top}\,\mathbf{e}_{z_{i-1}}^{(i)} & \text{revolute joint} \\ \mathbf{f}_{i,i-1}^{(i)\top}\,\mathbf{e}_{z_{i-1}}^{(i)} & \text{prismatic joint} \end{cases}$$
03

The Principle of Virtual Work

A holonomic system is at static equilibrium if and only if the total virtual work done by all active forces is zero for any virtual displacement.

δW
Principle of Virtual Work — General Statement
$$\delta W = \underbrace{\sum_i \tau_i\,\delta q_i}_{\text{joint torques}} + \underbrace{\sum_k \mathbf{F}_k\cdot\delta\mathbf{r}_k}_{\text{ext.\ forces}} + \underbrace{\sum_k \boldsymbol{\mu}_k\cdot\delta\boldsymbol{\phi}_k}_{\text{ext.\ couples}} = 0$$
03.2

Virtual Work Terms & the Jacobian

J
Virtual Work via Analytic Jacobian

Virtual work of joint torques $\boldsymbol{\tau}=[\tau_1,\ldots,\tau_n]^\top$:

$$\delta W_\tau = \boldsymbol{\tau}^\top\delta\mathbf{q}$$

Virtual displacement of end-effector via analytic Jacobian $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q})=[\mathbf{J}_T^\top,\;\mathbf{J}_R^\top]^\top$:

$$\delta\mathbf{r}_E = \mathbf{J}_T(\mathbf{q})\,\delta\mathbf{q},\qquad\delta\boldsymbol{\phi}_n = \mathbf{J}_R(\mathbf{q})\,\delta\mathbf{q}$$

Virtual work of external force $\mathbf{f}$ and torque $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ at the end-effector:

$$\delta W_E = \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q})\,\delta\mathbf{q}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\gamma}:=\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{f}\\\boldsymbol{\mu}\end{bmatrix}$$

Virtual work of gravity on all links ($\mathbf{r}_{C_i}^{(0)}$ — CoM of link $i$ in base frame):

$$\delta W_g = \sum_{i=1}^n m_i\,\mathbf{g}^{(0)\top}\mathbf{J}_{Ti}(\mathbf{q})\,\delta\mathbf{q}, \qquad \mathbf{J}_{Ti}=\frac{\partial\mathbf{r}_{C_i}^{(0)}}{\partial\mathbf{q}}$$
03.3

Static Equilibrium & Joint Torque Formula

★
Joint Torque Formula via Virtual Work

Setting total virtual work to zero for any $\delta\mathbf{q}$:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau}^\top + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q}) + \sum_{i=1}^n m_i\,\mathbf{g}^{(0)\top}\mathbf{J}_{Ti}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{0}$$

Solving for joint torques:

$$\boxed{\boldsymbol{\tau} = -\mathbf{J}^\top(\mathbf{q})\,\boldsymbol{\gamma} - \sum_{i=1}^n m_i\,\mathbf{J}_{Ti}^\top(\mathbf{q})\,\mathbf{g}^{(0)}}$$

where the first term handles the external wrench and the second handles gravity loading on all links.

💡

This single matrix equation replaces the entire recursive link-separation procedure — once the analytic Jacobian is known, joint torques follow immediately from a matrix–vector product.

03.4

Examples

Example 1 — RTR Planar Manipulator (massless links)

$\mathbf{q}=[q_1,q_2,q_3]^\top$, end-effector $\mathbf{x}_E=[x_e,y_e,\phi_e]^\top$

Forward kinematics (notation $s_{ij}=\sin(q_i+q_j)$, $c_{ij}=\cos(q_i+q_j)$):

$$x_e = -l_1 s_{q_1} + l_2 c_{q_{12}} + l_3 c_{q_{13}}, \qquad y_e = l_1 c_{q_1} + l_2 s_{q_{12}} + l_3 s_{q_{13}}$$

Analytic Jacobian $\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q})=\partial\mathbf{x}_E/\partial\mathbf{q}$:

$$\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix} -l_1c_{q_1}-l_2s_{q_{12}}-l_3s_{q_{13}} & -l_3s_{q_{13}} & -l_3s_{q_{13}} \\ -l_1s_{q_1}+l_2c_{q_{12}}+l_3c_{q_{13}} & l_3c_{q_{13}} & l_3c_{q_{13}} \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Joint torques with external wrench $\boldsymbol{\gamma}=[f_x,f_y,\mu]^\top$:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = -\mathbf{J}^\top(\mathbf{q})\,\boldsymbol{\gamma}$$
Example 2 — TR Spatial Manipulator (homogeneous rods)

$\mathbf{q}=[q_1,q_2]^\top$, revolute + prismatic joints, gravity included

$$\tau_1 = (m_1+m_2)g - f_{2,3,z}, \qquad \tau_2 = -\mu_{2,3}$$

Here $f_{2,3,z}$ and $\mu_{2,3}$ are the axial force and torque applied to link 2 at $O_2$.

04

Stiffness Analysis

Applied forces deform the robot's joints (transmission systems have finite stiffness), displacing the end-effector from its desired position. The Cartesian stiffness matrix maps force to deflection.

Joint compliance model — torsional deformation at each joint
04.2

Stiffness Matrix Derivation

K
Compliance and Stiffness Matrices

Joint compliance: $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \boldsymbol{\chi}\,\Delta\mathbf{q}$, where $\boldsymbol{\chi}=\mathrm{diag}(k_1,\ldots,k_n)$.

$$\Delta\mathbf{q} = \boldsymbol{\chi}^{-1}\boldsymbol{\tau} = \boldsymbol{\chi}^{-1}\mathbf{J}^\top\mathbf{f}$$

End-effector deflection via the differential kinematics $\Delta\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{J}\Delta\mathbf{q}$:

$$\Delta\mathbf{x} = \underbrace{\mathbf{J}\,\boldsymbol{\chi}^{-1}\,\mathbf{J}^\top}_{\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q})}\,\mathbf{f}$$

Compliance matrix $\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q})\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ (depends on configuration!):

$$\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q}) := \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{q})\,\boldsymbol{\chi}^{-1}\,\mathbf{J}^\top(\mathbf{q})$$

When $m=n$ and $\mathbf{J}$ is square non-singular, the Cartesian stiffness matrix:

$$\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{q}) := \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{J}^{-\top}\boldsymbol{\chi}\,\mathbf{J}^{-1}$$
⚠️

Both $\mathbf{C}(\mathbf{q})$ and $\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{q})$ depend on the robot configuration $\mathbf{q}$ through the Jacobian. The robot is stiffer in some directions and configurations than others — a critical consideration in precision tasks.

05

Statics of Parallel Robots — Structure Separation

A parallel robot has a moving platform connected to a fixed base by multiple legs. Each leg acts as a connecting rod transmitting tension/compression.

Parallel robot — free-body diagram of the moving platform
Λ
Platform Equilibrium — Matrix Form

Leg forces $\tau_k$ act along unit vectors $\mathbf{e}_k$. Let $\mathbf{r}_k = \overrightarrow{C_m B_k}$:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{6}\tau_k\,\mathbf{e}_k = -\mathbf{f}, \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{6}\mathbf{r}_k\times\tau_k\,\mathbf{e}_k = -\boldsymbol{\mu}$$

In matrix form (using $S(\mathbf{r}_k)\mathbf{e}_k = \mathbf{r}_k\times\mathbf{e}_k$):

$$\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{e}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{e}_6 \\ S(\mathbf{r}_1)\mathbf{e}_1 & \cdots & S(\mathbf{r}_6)\mathbf{e}_6\end{bmatrix}}_{\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\;\in\,\mathbb{R}^{6\times6}} \begin{bmatrix}\tau_1\\\vdots\\\tau_6\end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{f}\\\boldsymbol{\mu}\end{bmatrix}$$

If $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is non-singular, the actuator forces are uniquely determined.

Example — Planar 3RRR Delta Robot

Moving platform + 3 active links (connecting rods $S_1, S_2, S_3$)

Platform equilibrium (3 equations, 2D):

$$\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{e}_1 & \mathbf{e}_2 & \mathbf{e}_3 \\ (\mathbf{r}_1\times\mathbf{e}_1)_z & (\mathbf{r}_2\times\mathbf{e}_2)_z & (\mathbf{r}_3\times\mathbf{e}_3)_z\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}S_1\\S_2\\S_3\end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{f}\\\mu\end{bmatrix}$$

Once $S_k$ are found, each active link yields its motor torque:

$$\tau_k = -(\mathbf{e}_z\times S_k\,\mathbf{e}_k)\cdot\mathbf{a}_k, \quad k=1,2,3$$
05.2

Parallel Robots — Principle of Virtual Work

Let $\boldsymbol{\theta}=[\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3]^\top$ be active joint coordinates, $\mathbf{x}=[x,y,\varphi]^\top$ the platform pose, and $\boldsymbol{\gamma}=[f_x,f_y,\mu]^\top$ the external wrench.

δW
Virtual Work Formulation for Parallel Robots

Total virtual work:

$$\delta W = \boldsymbol{\tau}^\top\delta\boldsymbol{\theta} + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top\delta\mathbf{x} = 0$$

Geometric constraints $\mathbf{g}(\boldsymbol{\theta},\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{0}$ differentiated:

$$\mathbf{G}_\theta\,\delta\boldsymbol{\theta} + \mathbf{G}_x\,\delta\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \delta\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{G}_x^{-1}\mathbf{G}_\theta\,\delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

Substituting and factoring out $\delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$:

$$\left(\boldsymbol{\tau}^\top - \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top\mathbf{G}_x^{-1}\mathbf{G}_\theta\right)\delta\boldsymbol{\theta} = 0 \quad\forall\,\delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

Actuator torques:

$$\boxed{\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{G}_\theta^\top(\boldsymbol{\theta},\mathbf{x})\,\mathbf{G}_x^{-\top}(\boldsymbol{\theta},\mathbf{x})\,\boldsymbol{\gamma}}$$
ℹ️

The matrices $\mathbf{G}_\theta$ and $\mathbf{G}_x$ are the Jacobians of the geometric loop-closure constraints with respect to active joint and platform coordinates. They replace the end-effector Jacobian used in serial-robot analysis.
